Byrne v Southern and Western Ry Co Court of Appeal, February 1884:
The plaintiff was a superintendent of the telegraph office at Limerick Junction. His office consisted of a small building at one end of the railway. At one point, due to the negligence of another worker, a train broke down the wall of the office. Although the superintendent was not hurt, he himself claimed that ‘a hair on his head was not touched’, he did get a great fright and a shock. He heard shouts of the clerks and believed that the office was all going to collapse.


In this case it was held that as the injury was reasonably foreseeable the plaintiff was allowed to recover. Contrast this case with the case of Victoria Ry Commrs v Coultas (1888) 13 AC 222 (notes 6.1).Byrne was later confirmed in the case of: Bell v G.N. Ry. Co. 26 L.R. (Ir) 428
